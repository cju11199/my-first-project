# Procedures — Treatment Sites & Tumors

_Original practice questions for ARRT Radiation Therapy registry review. Not actual exam items; not affiliated with the ARRT._

## CNS

### SIT-01 · _recall_
For newly diagnosed glioblastoma, which combined regimen is most commonly used following maximal safe resection?

- **A.** 60 Gy in 30 fractions with concurrent and adjuvant temozolomide
- **B.** 30 Gy in 10 fractions alone
- **C.** Single-fraction stereotactic radiosurgery of 18 Gy
- **D.** 45 Gy in 25 fractions with cisplatin

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** The Stupp regimen pairs fractionated radiotherapy of about 60 Gy in 30 fractions with concurrent and adjuvant temozolomide as a commonly used standard for glioblastoma.
**Source:** NCCN Central Nervous System Cancers

### SIT-02 · _application_
When contouring a glioblastoma target, the planning team includes the contrast-enhancing tumor plus the surrounding FLAIR/T2 signal abnormality with an added margin. The primary rationale is that this region most likely contains:

- **A.** Radioresistant calcified scar tissue
- **B.** Normal cortex that should be intentionally treated
- **C.** Microscopic infiltrative tumor extension beyond the enhancing mass
- **D.** Only post-surgical blood products

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Glioblastoma spreads by infiltration along white matter tracts, so the CTV adds the peritumoral FLAIR/edema region plus margin to cover likely microscopic disease.
**Source:** NCCN Central Nervous System Cancers

### SIT-03 · _recall_
A small vestibular schwannoma (acoustic neuroma) treated with single-fraction stereotactic radiosurgery is typically prescribed approximately:

- **A.** 12–13 Gy in a single fraction
- **B.** 60 Gy in 30 fractions
- **C.** 45 Gy in 25 fractions
- **D.** 8 Gy in a single fraction

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Vestibular schwannomas are commonly managed with SRS in the range of about 12–13 Gy in one fraction to control growth while limiting hearing loss and facial/trigeminal nerve injury.
**Source:** NCCN Central Nervous System Cancers

### SIT-04 · _application_
A patient with four small brain metastases and good systemic disease control is referred for radiotherapy. Compared with whole-brain radiation therapy, stereotactic radiosurgery is generally preferred in this setting because it:

- **A.** Always delivers a lower total tumor dose
- **B.** Eliminates the need for any imaging follow-up
- **C.** Is the only option when there is leptomeningeal disease
- **D.** Better spares normal brain and reduces neurocognitive decline for limited metastases

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** For a limited number of brain metastases, SRS focuses high dose on each lesion while sparing uninvolved brain, lowering neurocognitive toxicity compared with whole-brain RT (commonly about 30 Gy in 10 fractions).
**Source:** NCCN Central Nervous System Cancers

## Head & Neck

### SIT-05 · _recall_
Which factor most strongly changes the staging and prognosis of oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma?

- **A.** Epstein–Barr virus status
- **B.** Human papillomavirus (HPV/p16) status
- **C.** Hepatitis C status
- **D.** Cytomegalovirus status

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** HPV/p16-positive oropharyngeal cancers carry a markedly better prognosis and are staged separately from HPV-negative disease in AJCC 8th edition.
**Source:** AJCC 8th ed.

A commonly used definitive regimen for locally advanced head and neck squamous cell carcinoma is approximately:

- **B.** 20 Gy in 5 fractions alone
- **C.** 30 Gy in 10 fractions with temozolomide
- **D.** 45 Gy in 25 fractions with brachytherapy boost to point A

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Definitive chemoradiation for locally advanced head and neck SCC commonly delivers about 70 Gy in 35 fractions with concurrent cisplatin.
**Source:** NCCN Head and Neck Cancers

### SIT-07 · _application_
A patient with a left tonsil primary has clinically negative neck nodes. When designing elective nodal coverage, the therapist expects the first-echelon drainage to involve cervical levels:

- **A.** I and Ia only
- **B.** VII only
- **C.** Supraclavicular fossa only
- **D.** II–IV (with possible retropharyngeal)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Oropharyngeal primaries drain predominantly to the upper and mid jugular chain (levels II–IV), with retropharyngeal nodes also at risk.
**Source:** NCCN Head and Neck Cancers

### SIT-08 · _recall_
Nasopharyngeal carcinoma is classically associated with which virus?

- **A.** Human papillomavirus
- **B.** Human immunodeficiency virus
- **C.** Herpes simplex virus
- **D.** Epstein–Barr virus

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Nasopharyngeal carcinoma, especially the non-keratinizing type, is strongly linked to Epstein–Barr virus infection.
**Source:** AJCC 8th ed.

### SIT-09 · _application_
A patient with an early (T1) glottic larynx cancer chooses radiotherapy for voice preservation. A commonly used regimen targets only the involved larynx with a dose of approximately:

- **A.** 70 Gy plus bilateral neck irradiation
- **B.** 8 Gy single fraction
- **C.** 45 Gy with concurrent cisplatin to the whole neck
- **D.** 63–66 Gy in conventional fractionation to the larynx

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Early glottic cancers have a very low rate of nodal spread, so treatment is confined to the larynx using about 63–66 Gy to preserve voice quality without elective neck RT.
**Source:** NCCN Head and Neck Cancers

## Thorax

### SIT-10 · _recall_
A commonly used definitive regimen for unresectable locally advanced non-small cell lung cancer is approximately:

- **A.** 60 Gy in 30 fractions with concurrent chemotherapy
- **B.** 25 Gy in 10 fractions alone
- **C.** 12 Gy single fraction
- **D.** 45 Gy adjuvant with no chemotherapy

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Definitive management of stage III NSCLC commonly uses about 60 Gy in 30 fractions with concurrent chemotherapy, often followed by consolidation immunotherapy.
**Source:** NCCN Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer

### SIT-11 · _application_
A medically inoperable patient has a 2 cm peripheral stage I NSCLC nodule. The most appropriate radiotherapy approach is stereotactic body radiation therapy, characterized by:

- **A.** Many low-dose fractions over six weeks
- **B.** A few high-dose fractions (for example 50 Gy in 5) with tight margins
- **C.** Elective whole-mediastinum irradiation
- **D.** Prophylactic cranial irradiation

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** SBRT delivers a few high-dose, conformal fractions (such as ~50 Gy in 5) to a small peripheral early-stage tumor, achieving high local control in inoperable patients.
**Source:** NCCN Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer

### SIT-12 · _recall_
For limited-stage small cell lung cancer, the standard radiotherapy approach is:

- **A.** Radiation alone with no chemotherapy
- **B.** Concurrent chemoradiation to the thorax
- **C.** Single-fraction SBRT
- **D.** Brachytherapy to the bronchus

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Limited-stage SCLC is treated with concurrent chemoradiation to the thoracic disease, frequently using an accelerated thoracic RT schedule.
**Source:** NCCN Small Cell Lung Cancer

### SIT-13 · _application_
A patient with limited-stage SCLC achieves a good response to chemoradiation. The radiation oncologist recommends prophylactic cranial irradiation (about 25 Gy in 10 fractions) because:

- **A.** It cures the primary thoracic tumor
- **B.** The brain is a common site of subclinical metastatic spread in SCLC
- **C.** It replaces the need for thoracic radiation
- **D.** SCLC never spreads to the brain

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** SCLC has a high propensity for occult brain metastases, so PCI (commonly ~25 Gy in 10 fractions) is offered to responders to reduce intracranial relapse.
**Source:** NCCN Small Cell Lung Cancer

## Breast

### SIT-14 · _recall_
Following lumpectomy for early-stage invasive ductal carcinoma, a commonly used moderately hypofractionated whole-breast regimen is approximately:

- **A.** 70 Gy in 35 fractions
- **B.** 8 Gy in a single fraction
- **C.** 60 Gy in 30 fractions
- **D.** 40–42.5 Gy in 15–16 fractions

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Moderate hypofractionation of about 40–42.5 Gy in 15–16 fractions is a widely used whole-breast standard, with conventional 50 Gy in 25 fractions as an alternative; a tumor-bed boost is often added.
**Source:** NCCN Breast Cancer

### SIT-15 · _application_
A patient with left-sided breast cancer is set up for whole-breast radiation. The team uses deep inspiration breath-hold primarily to:

- **A.** Reduce skin dose to the contralateral breast
- **B.** Increase the distance between the heart and the chest wall, lowering cardiac dose
- **C.** Shorten the overall treatment time per fraction to seconds
- **D.** Eliminate the need for a bolus

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** DIBH expands the lungs and displaces the heart posteriorly/inferiorly away from the left chest wall, reducing cardiac and LAD coronary artery dose.
**Source:** NCCN Breast Cancer

### SIT-16 · _recall_
When regional nodal irradiation is indicated for breast cancer, the axillary nodal levels treated are described as levels I–III in relation to which muscle?

- **A.** Sternocleidomastoid
- **B.** Latissimus dorsi
- **C.** Serratus anterior
- **D.** Pectoralis minor

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Axillary levels are defined by the pectoralis minor: level I is lateral, level II is behind, and level III is medial to it; internal mammary and supraclavicular nodes may also be targeted.
**Source:** AJCC 8th ed.

### SIT-17 · _application_
A patient presents with diffuse erythema, skin edema (peau d'orange), and rapid onset over the breast, consistent with inflammatory breast cancer (T4d). Standard local management typically includes:

- **A.** Mastectomy followed by post-mastectomy radiation including regional nodes
- **B.** Breast-conserving surgery with no radiation
- **C.** Observation only
- **D.** Single-fraction radiosurgery

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Inflammatory breast cancer (T4d) is managed with neoadjuvant chemotherapy, mastectomy, and post-mastectomy radiation that covers the chest wall and regional nodes.
**Source:** NCCN Breast Cancer

In prostate cancer, tumor grade is now expressed using the Grade Group system derived from the:

- **A.** PSA velocity
- **B.** Clinical T stage only
- **C.** Gleason score
- **D.** Bone scan findings

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The Gleason score (sum of the two most prevalent patterns) is grouped into Grade Groups 1–5, which stratify prognosis and risk.
**Source:** AJCC 8th ed.

### SIT-23 · _recall_
A commonly used conventionally fractionated definitive dose for prostate cancer is approximately:

- **A.** 78–80 Gy
- **B.** 45 Gy
- **C.** 30 Gy in 10 fractions
- **D.** 8 Gy single fraction

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Conventional definitive prostate RT commonly delivers about 78–80 Gy, with moderate hypofractionation and SBRT (a few high-dose fractions) as accepted alternatives.
**Source:** NCCN Prostate Cancer

### SIT-24 · _application_
A prostate cancer patient has gold fiducial markers implanted before radiotherapy. Their primary purpose is to:

- **A.** Increase the radiation dose by acting as a bolus
- **B.** Treat the rectum and bladder simultaneously
- **C.** Replace the need for a treatment plan
- **D.** Enable accurate daily image-guided localization of the prostate target

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Gold fiducials provide high-contrast landmarks for daily kV image matching, correcting for prostate motion; the rectum and bladder are key organs at risk to spare.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver

### SIT-25 · _application_
A patient with intermediate- or high-risk prostate cancer is frequently treated with androgen-deprivation therapy in addition to radiotherapy because it:

- **A.** Improves disease control by suppressing testosterone-driven tumor growth
- **B.** Eliminates the need for image guidance
- **C.** Lowers the prescription dose to 20 Gy
- **D.** Prevents bladder filling

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** ADT lowers androgens that drive prostate cancer growth and, combined with RT, improves outcomes in intermediate- and high-risk disease.
**Source:** NCCN Prostate Cancer

### SIT-26 · _application_
A patient with muscle-invasive bladder cancer who wishes to avoid cystectomy is offered bladder-preservation therapy. This tri-modality approach combines maximal transurethral resection with:

- **A.** Radiosurgery alone
- **B.** Concurrent chemotherapy and radiation
- **C.** Hormone therapy alone
- **D.** Observation

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Tri-modality bladder preservation uses maximal TURBT followed by concurrent chemoradiation, reserving cystectomy for non-responders.
**Source:** NCCN Bladder Cancer

### SIT-27 · _recall_
Renal cell carcinoma is traditionally considered relatively radioresistant to conventional fractionation, but radiation is increasingly used as:

- **A.** SBRT for oligometastatic or selected primary lesions
- **B.** Standard adjuvant whole-abdomen RT
- **C.** Prophylactic cranial irradiation
- **D.** Single 2 Gy fractions over six weeks

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** RCC responds poorly to low-dose-per-fraction RT but is sensitive to the high doses per fraction delivered by SBRT, used for oligometastases and some primary tumors.
**Source:** NCCN Kidney Cancer

## Sarcoma

A commonly used neoadjuvant (preoperative) radiation dose for extremity soft-tissue sarcoma is approximately:

- **A.** 50 Gy
- **B.** 78 Gy
- **C.** 8 Gy single fraction
- **D.** 25 Gy in 5 fractions to the whole limb

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Preoperative RT for soft-tissue sarcoma commonly delivers about 50 Gy; adjuvant (postoperative) treatment typically uses a higher total of about 60–66 Gy.

### SIT-36 · _application_
A patient with a high-grade extremity soft-tissue sarcoma is being staged. The therapist anticipates that the most common site of distant spread is the:

- **A.** Regional lymph nodes
- **B.** Liver
- **C.** Bone marrow
- **D.** Lungs (hematogenous)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Soft-tissue sarcomas spread hematogenously, most commonly to the lungs; lymph node involvement is uncommon for most histologies.
**Source:** AJCC 8th ed.

### SIT-37 · _application_
When choosing preoperative versus postoperative radiation for soft-tissue sarcoma, preoperative RT generally uses a smaller field and lower dose but is associated with a higher rate of:

- **A.** Cardiac toxicity
- **B.** Hearing loss
- **C.** Acute wound-healing complications
- **D.** Xerostomia

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Preoperative RT (smaller volume, ~50 Gy) trades a higher acute wound-complication rate for lower late fibrosis compared with higher-dose postoperative RT.
**Source:** NCCN Soft Tissue Sarcoma

### SIT-45 · _application_
A patient with prostate cancer develops vertebral metastases without a clear lung metastatic phase. This pattern of direct spread to the spine is classically attributed to the:

- **A.** Thoracic duct
- **B.** Lymphatic levels I–III
- **C.** Portal venous system
- **D.** Batson (vertebral) venous plexus

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The valveless Batson vertebral venous plexus allows pelvic tumors such as prostate cancer to seed the vertebral column directly, explaining bone metastases that bypass the lungs.
**Source:** Gunderson & Tepper
