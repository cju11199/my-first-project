# Safety — Radiation Protection, Equipment Operation & Quality Assurance

_Original practice questions for ARRT Radiation Therapy registry review. Not actual exam items; not affiliated with the ARRT._

## ALARA and the Three Protection Tools

### RPQ-01 · _recall_
The ALARA principle is best summarized by which of the following statements?

- **A.** Doses must be held to exactly the regulatory limit
- **B.** Only the patient dose needs to be minimized
- **C.** Shielding alone satisfies all radiation-safety requirements
- **D.** Doses should be kept as low as reasonably achievable below the limits

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** ALARA (As Low As Reasonably Achievable) directs that exposures be minimized below regulatory limits using economic and social factors, not merely held at the legal ceiling.
**Source:** CDC ALARA

### RPQ-02 · _recall_
Which three operational tools form the foundation of external radiation protection?

- **A.** Time, distance, and shielding
- **B.** Filtration, collimation, and grids
- **C.** Calibration, interlocks, and surveys
- **D.** Dosimetry, signage, and training

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Reducing time near a source, increasing distance, and adding shielding are the three classic cardinal principles of external exposure reduction.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-03 · _application_
A therapist must spend time near a sealed source. Cutting the exposure time in half will change the received dose how?

- **A.** Double it
- **B.** Halve it
- **C.** Quarter it
- **D.** Leave it unchanged

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** For a constant dose rate, accumulated dose is directly proportional to time, so halving time halves dose.
**Source:** CDC ALARA

### RPQ-04 · _recall_
Of the three cardinal protection tools, which is generally the most effective per unit effort for a point source because of its squared relationship to dose rate?

- **A.** Distance
- **B.** Time
- **C.** Shielding
- **D.** Filtration

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Distance follows the inverse-square law, so modest increases in distance produce large reductions in dose rate from a point source.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-05 · _application_
Which action represents the "distance" tool rather than the "shielding" tool?

- **A.** Standing behind the control-area maze wall
- **B.** Stepping back several feet from a source during a survey
- **C.** Wearing a lead apron in fluoroscopy
- **D.** Adding concrete to a treatment-room wall

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Increasing the separation between worker and source is the distance tool; the others interpose absorbing material (shielding).
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-06 · _calculation_
At 1 m from a point source the exposure rate is 80 mGy/h. Using the inverse-square law, what is the rate at 4 m?

- **A.** 20 mGy/h
- **B.** 10 mGy/h
- **C.** 2.5 mGy/h
- **D.** 5 mGy/h

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Rate scales as 1/d²: 80 × (1/4)² = 80 × 1/16 = 5 mGy/h.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-07 · _calculation_
An exposure rate is 36 mGy/h at 2 m from a point source. At what distance would the rate fall to 4 mGy/h?

- **A.** 6 m
- **B.** 3 m
- **C.** 9 m
- **D.** 18 m

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** I₁d₁² = I₂d₂², so d₂ = √(36×2²/4) = √36 = 6 m.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-08 · _calculation_
A source reads 100 µGy/h at 50 cm. What is the approximate rate at 200 cm?

- **A.** 25 µGy/h
- **B.** 6.25 µGy/h
- **C.** 12.5 µGy/h
- **D.** 50 µGy/h

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Distance increases by a factor of 4, so the rate drops by 4² = 16: 100/16 ≈ 6.25 µGy/h.
**Source:** NCRP-151

## Dose Limits — Occupational, Public, and Special

### RPQ-09 · _recall_
Under US NRC 10 CFR 20, what is the annual occupational whole-body effective dose limit?

- **A.** 5 mSv/yr
- **B.** 20 mSv/yr
- **C.** 150 mSv/yr
- **D.** 50 mSv/yr

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** 10 CFR 20 sets the occupational total effective dose equivalent limit at 50 mSv (5 rem) per year.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The public dose limit under 10 CFR 20 is 1 mSv (100 mrem) per year above background.

A monitored therapist's badge reads 4 mSv for the year. Relative to the 50 mSv occupational limit, this result is:

## Facility Shielding Design

### RPQ-20 · _recall_
A primary barrier in a linac vault is designed to attenuate which radiation?

- **A.** Scattered radiation only
- **B.** The useful (primary) beam
- **C.** Leakage radiation only
- **D.** Neutron contamination only

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Primary barriers attenuate the useful beam directed at them; secondary barriers handle scatter and leakage.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-21 · _recall_
A secondary barrier is designed primarily to attenuate:

- **A.** The useful primary beam
- **B.** Only neutrons
- **C.** Visible light from the lasers
- **D.** Leakage and scattered radiation

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Secondary barriers protect against leakage through the head and radiation scattered from the patient and surfaces, which are lower in intensity than the primary beam.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-22 · _recall_
In shielding calculations, the workload (W) most directly represents:

- **A.** The number of staff in the area
- **B.** The occupancy time of an adjacent room
- **C.** The fraction of beam-on time aimed at a barrier
- **D.** The radiation output use per week (e.g., cGy at 1 m per week)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Workload W quantifies the machine's weekly radiation output (commonly cGy at 1 m per week), driving barrier thickness.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-23 · _recall_
The use factor (U) in barrier design refers to:

- **A.** The fraction of time a person occupies the protected area
- **B.** The fraction of time the primary beam is directed at a given barrier
- **C.** The total weekly beam output
- **D.** The number of half-value layers required

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The use (beam-direction) factor U is the fraction of the workload during which the primary beam points toward the barrier in question.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-24 · _recall_
The occupancy factor (T) describes:

- **A.** The average fraction of time the area beyond a barrier is occupied
- **B.** The fraction of time the beam is on
- **C.** The patient's position on the couch
- **D.** The leakage allowance of the linac head

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Occupancy factor T is the fraction of time the adjacent area is occupied by the maximally exposed individual.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-25 · _application_
A storage closet that is rarely entered would be assigned which kind of occupancy factor compared with a full-time office?

- **A.** A higher occupancy factor
- **B.** The same occupancy factor
- **C.** A lower occupancy factor
- **D.** An occupancy factor of exactly 1

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Infrequently occupied spaces receive a low T (e.g., 1/20–1/40), whereas a continuously occupied office is assigned T = 1.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-26 · _recall_
A "controlled area" differs from an "uncontrolled area" in that the controlled area is:

- **A.** Open to the general public without restriction
- **B.** Limited to the 1 mSv/yr public limit

- **D.** Never adjacent to a treatment vault

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Controlled areas have managed access and are designed to occupational limits; uncontrolled areas are designed to the public limit.

### RPQ-27 · _application_
The design dose-equivalent goal beyond a barrier separating the vault from a public corridor should be based on which limit?

- **A.** 50 mSv/yr occupational
- **B.** 5 mSv gestational
- **C.** 150 mSv/yr lens
- **D.** 1 mSv/yr public

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A public corridor is an uncontrolled area, so barriers are designed to keep dose at or below the 1 mSv/yr public limit (often with weekly design goals).

### RPQ-28 · _recall_
High-energy linac vaults often use concrete plus a localized high-Z material at primary barriers. That high-Z material is typically:

- **A.** Lead or steel
- **B.** Aluminum
- **C.** Plastic
- **D.** Copper foil

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Lead or steel is added where space is limited because its high density/atomic number attenuates photons more efficiently per unit thickness than concrete.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-29 · _calculation_
If a barrier material has a tenth-value layer (TVL) such that each TVL reduces intensity to 1/10, how many TVLs are needed to reduce the beam to 1/1000 of its original intensity?

- **A.** 2
- **B.** 4
- **C.** 3
- **D.** 10

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Each TVL multiplies by 0.1; (0.1)³ = 0.001 = 1/1000, so 3 TVLs are required.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-30 · _calculation_
A beam passes through 5 half-value layers (HVLs) of shielding. What fraction of the original intensity is transmitted?

- **A.** 1/32
- **B.** 1/10
- **C.** 1/16
- **D.** 1/64

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Transmission after n HVLs is (1/2)ⁿ; (1/2)⁵ = 1/32.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-31 · _calculation_
For a linac vault, why are neutron considerations relevant only above a certain beam energy?

- **A.** Neutrons appear at all photon energies equally
- **B.** Neutrons only occur below 6 MV
- **C.** Neutrons are produced by the imaging kV source
- **D.** Neutrons are produced by photonuclear reactions above roughly 10 MV

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Photoneutron production becomes significant above about 10 MV, requiring hydrogenous shielding (e.g., borated polyethylene) in mazes/doors.
**Source:** NCRP-151

## Personnel Dosimetry

### RPQ-32 · _recall_
A thermoluminescent dosimeter (TLD) commonly uses which material and offers what key advantage?

- **A.** Silver-halide film; permanent legal record
- **B.** Aluminum oxide; instant digital readout only
- **C.** Geiger tube; real-time alarms
- **D.** Lithium fluoride (LiF); reusable after readout

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** TLDs use LiF, which is near-tissue-equivalent and reusable because heating releases the stored signal and resets the crystal.
**Source:** NCRP-151

## Linac Components and Beam Production

### RPQ-39 · _recall_
In a medical linac, the component that supplies the initial electrons for acceleration is the:

- **A.** Tungsten target
- **B.** Electron gun
- **C.** Flattening filter
- **D.** Primary collimator

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The electron gun injects electrons into the accelerating waveguide where microwave power accelerates them.

### RPQ-40 · _recall_
In photon mode, x-rays are produced when the accelerated electron beam strikes the:

- **A.** Flattening filter
- **B.** Monitor ionization chamber
- **C.** Multileaf collimator
- **D.** High-Z target (e.g., tungsten)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Bremsstrahlung x-rays are generated when high-energy electrons strike a high-Z target such as tungsten.
**Source:** AAPM Reports (TG-142)

### RPQ-41 · _recall_
The conventional flattening filter is used to:

- **A.** Generate the electron beam
- **B.** Monitor dose output
- **C.** Produce a uniform photon fluence across the field
- **D.** Collimate the field edges

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The flattening filter shapes the forward-peaked bremsstrahlung into a flat (uniform) beam profile across the field.
**Source:** AAPM Reports (TG-142)

### RPQ-42 · _recall_
A flattening-filter-free (FFF) beam is characterized by:

- **A.** A forward-peaked profile and high dose rate
- **B.** A flat profile and low dose rate
- **C.** Only electron output
- **D.** No need for monitor chambers

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Removing the flattening filter yields a higher dose rate with a forward-peaked (non-flat) profile, useful for SRS/SBRT with shorter beam-on times.
**Source:** AAPM Reports (TG-142)

### RPQ-43 · _recall_
The dual monitor ionization chambers in a linac head primarily serve to:

- **A.** Continuously monitor and terminate the dose (MU) with redundancy
- **B.** Bend the electron beam
- **C.** Flatten the photon beam
- **D.** Generate the imaging kV beam

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Sealed, redundant transmission ionization chambers measure delivered MU and terminate the beam; the second chamber provides a backup if the first fails.
**Source:** AAPM Reports (TG-142)

### RPQ-44 · _recall_
The collimating jaws and the multileaf collimator (MLC) together function to:

- **A.** Accelerate electrons
- **B.** Measure HU values
- **C.** Define and shape the treatment field
- **D.** Provide door interlocks

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Jaws set the rectangular field boundaries and the MLC conforms the field to the target shape for conformal/IMRT delivery.

### RPQ-45 · _application_
For a clinical electron beam, the high-Z target is retracted and replaced in the beam path with:

**Explanation:** Water should read 0 HU (typically within a few HU); +18 HU exceeds typical tolerance and indicates a calibration check is needed before use in planning.
**Source:** AAPM Reports (TG-66)

## Survey Meters and Radiation Detection

### RPQ-63 · _recall_
An ionization-chamber survey meter is generally preferred over a Geiger-Müller (GM) counter for:

- **A.** Detecting very low-level contamination
- **B.** Finding a lost low-activity seed quickly
- **C.** Measuring CT numbers
- **D.** Accurate measurement of exposure rate in higher-intensity fields

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Ion-chamber meters give accurate exposure/dose-rate readings over a wide range, while GM counters are more sensitive for low-level detection but can saturate at high rates.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-64 · _recall_
A Geiger-Müller (GM) survey meter is best suited for:

- **A.** Sensitive detection of low-level contamination or a lost source
- **B.** Precise exposure-rate measurement in a megavoltage beam
- **C.** Measuring HU linearity
- **D.** Calibrating monitor units

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** GM detectors are highly sensitive and ideal for locating low-level contamination or a missing source, though not for accurate high-rate dosimetry.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-65 · _application_
Before using any survey meter for an area survey, the operator should first:

- **A.** Assume it is functioning and proceed
- **B.** Verify calibration is current and perform a battery/response (source) check
- **C.** Disable the audible alarm
- **D.** Recalibrate the linac

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A meter must have a current calibration and pass a pre-use battery and response check (e.g., against a known check source) to give trustworthy readings.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-66 · _application_
After a high-dose-rate or sealed-source procedure, a survey of the patient/area is performed primarily to:

- **A.** Measure the CT number of the room
- **B.** Verify the source has been fully returned/removed and no residual radiation remains
- **C.** Calibrate the dosimeters
- **D.** Test the door interlock

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A post-procedure survey confirms the source is accounted for and no unexpected radiation field remains before releasing the patient or area.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-67 · _application_
A survey meter reads elevated levels at a vault door during beam-on that exceed the design goal. The first appropriate response is to:

- **A.** Continue treatments and resurvey next year
- **B.** Increase the workload factor
- **C.** Replace the patient dosimeter
- **D.** Halt use, restrict access, and notify the RSO/physics for investigation

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Readings exceeding the shielding design goal require stopping use, controlling access, and notifying the radiation safety officer/physics to investigate the barrier.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-68 · _recall_
Posting a "Caution: Radiation Area" sign is required where dose rates could result in a person receiving more than a specified level in a defined time. This requirement is part of:

- **A.** Voluntary best practice only
- **B.** The flattening-filter design
- **C.** The MTF specification
- **D.** Regulatory area-posting requirements

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Area posting (Radiation Area, High Radiation Area, etc.) is a regulatory control to warn workers and the public of potential exposure.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### RPQ-69 · _calculation_
A worker spends 30 minutes per week at a location where the dose rate is 0.02 mSv/h. What is the approximate annual dose (50 weeks)?

- **A.** 1 mSv
- **B.** 5 mSv
- **C.** 0.5 mSv
- **D.** 10 mSv

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** 0.02 mSv/h × 0.5 h/week × 50 weeks = 0.5 mSv/yr, well within occupational limits.
**Source:** NCRP-151

### RPQ-70 · _application_
The most fundamental purpose of integrating ALARA, dose limits, shielding, dosimetry, and QA programs in a radiation oncology department is to:

- **A.** Maximize machine output
- **B.** Eliminate the need for survey meters
- **C.** Protect patients, staff, and the public while delivering accurate treatment
- **D.** Replace regulatory limits with internal goals

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Collectively these programs ensure safe, accurate radiation delivery while keeping exposures to patients, staff, and the public as low as reasonably achievable within legal limits.
**Source:** CDC ALARA
