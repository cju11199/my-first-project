# Procedures — Treatments

_Original practice questions for ARRT Radiation Therapy registry review. Not actual exam items; not affiliated with the ARRT._

## External-Beam Techniques

### TRX-01 · _recall_
Which planning technique shapes multiple static beams to the target using a multileaf collimator but delivers each field at a uniform intensity?

- **A.** 3D-CRT
- **B.** IMRT
- **C.** VMAT
- **D.** Tomotherapy

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** 3D-conformal radiation therapy (3D-CRT) uses beam's-eye-view shaping with the MLC or blocks, but each field is delivered at a flat, uniform fluence rather than a modulated one.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-02 · _recall_
The defining feature of intensity-modulated radiation therapy (IMRT) compared with 3D-CRT is that IMRT:

- **A.** Varies the fluence across each treatment field
- **B.** Always uses a single rotating arc
- **C.** Eliminates the need for a treatment-planning system
- **D.** Uses only electron beams

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** IMRT modulates the photon fluence within each field, producing non-uniform intensity maps that allow concave dose distributions and improved sparing of adjacent organs at risk.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-03 · _recall_
In step-and-shoot IMRT delivery, the multileaf collimator leaves move:

- **A.** Continuously while the beam is on
- **B.** Only between segments, with the beam off during leaf motion
- **C.** Synchronously with continuous gantry rotation
- **D.** Independently of the dose-rate setting

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Step-and-shoot (segmental) IMRT delivers a series of static MLC-shaped segments; the beam is turned off while the leaves reposition between segments, then turned on for each static aperture.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-04 · _recall_
Sliding-window IMRT differs from step-and-shoot IMRT in that sliding-window delivery:

- **A.** Cannot be planned with inverse optimization
- **B.** Uses no multileaf collimator
- **C.** Keeps the beam on while the MLC leaves move continuously across the field
- **D.** Requires a dedicated cobalt-60 unit

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Sliding-window (dynamic MLC) IMRT keeps the beam on while opposing leaf pairs sweep across the field at varying speeds, modulating fluence continuously rather than in discrete static segments.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-05 · _recall_
Volumetric-modulated arc therapy (VMAT) is best characterized by:

- **A.** Continuous dose delivery during gantry rotation with simultaneous MLC, dose-rate, and gantry-speed variation
- **B.** Multiple fixed gantry angles with static MLC shapes
- **C.** Electron arcs for superficial chest wall targets
- **D.** A stationary gantry with a rotating couch only

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** VMAT delivers a modulated dose during one or more continuous gantry arcs, varying MLC leaf positions, dose rate, and gantry speed simultaneously, which typically makes treatment faster than fixed-field IMRT.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-06 · _application_
A clinician wants to reduce a head-and-neck patient's daily beam-on time while preserving a highly conformal, concave dose around the spinal cord. Compared with seven-field static IMRT, which approach most directly achieves the shorter delivery time?

- **A.** 3D-CRT
- **B.** Single anterior electron field
- **C.** Parallel-opposed photon fields
- **D.** VMAT

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** VMAT delivers comparable concave, conformal distributions to fixed-field IMRT but in one or two continuous arcs, generally shortening beam-on and overall delivery time.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-07 · _recall_
A hallmark of stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS) and stereotactic body radiation therapy (SBRT) is:

- **A.** Many small fractions over six to eight weeks
- **B.** A few fractions delivering a high dose per fraction with rigid immobilization
- **C.** Whole-body low-dose irradiation
- **D.** Reliance on loose, comfort-only positioning

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** SRS/SBRT deliver ablative doses in one to about five fractions with a high dose per fraction, demanding rigid immobilization and image guidance to achieve roughly 1–2 mm targeting precision.
**Source:** AAPM TG-101

### TRX-08 · _recall_
The setup precision typically required for cranial SRS is on the order of:

- **A.** 1–2 mm
- **B.** 1–2 cm
- **C.** 5–10 mm
- **D.** 3–5 cm

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Because of the high dose per fraction and small margins, SRS demands sub-millimeter-to-roughly-1–2 mm targeting accuracy, achieved through rigid frames or frameless systems plus image guidance.
**Source:** AAPM TG-101

### TRX-09 · _application_
A patient with a single small brain metastasis is scheduled for single-fraction treatment to a high ablative dose using a rigid head frame and sub-millimeter positioning. This describes:

- **A.** SRS
- **B.** Conventional whole-brain radiotherapy
- **C.** Total body irradiation
- **D.** Electron-beam therapy

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** A single-fraction, high-dose, rigidly immobilized intracranial treatment with sub-millimeter precision is stereotactic radiosurgery (SRS).
**Source:** AAPM TG-101

### TRX-10 · _recall_
Why is rigid immobilization especially critical for SBRT?

- **A.** The dose per fraction is low, so motion is irrelevant
- **B.** It allows the patient to move freely during treatment
- **C.** Large dose-per-fraction and tight margins make small setup errors dosimetrically significant
- **D.** It is required only for electron beams

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** With high dose per fraction and minimal margins, even a few millimeters of motion can underdose the target or overdose adjacent normal tissue, so rigid immobilization and motion management are essential.
**Source:** AAPM TG-101

### TRX-11 · _recall_
Total body irradiation (TBI) as conditioning before bone-marrow or stem-cell transplant commonly uses a regimen on the order of:

- **A.** 80 Gy in 40 fractions
- **B.** 12 Gy in roughly 6 fractions
- **C.** 2 Gy single fraction
- **D.** 50 Gy in 25 fractions

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A commonly used myeloablative TBI schedule is approximately 12 Gy delivered in about six fractions (often twice daily), though exact prescriptions are protocol-dependent.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-12 · _recall_
A shielding consideration unique to TBI delivery is the use of:

- **A.** Lung shielding (partial transmission blocks) to limit pulmonary dose
- **B.** A rigid stereotactic head frame
- **C.** Tandem and ovoid applicators
- **D.** Iodine-125 seeds

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Because the lungs are dose-limiting in TBI, partial-transmission lung blocks (compensators) are commonly used to keep pulmonary dose below tolerance and reduce the risk of radiation pneumonitis.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-13 · _application_
A leukemia patient is prescribed bilateral large fields covering the entire body at extended SSD prior to allogeneic transplant. The therapist places partial-transmission blocks over the lung regions. The primary rationale is to:

- **A.** Reduce the risk of radiation pneumonitis by limiting lung dose
- **B.** Increase dose to the marrow
- **C.** Improve skin-sparing over the abdomen
- **D.** Shorten the treatment time

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Lung tissue tolerance is the principal constraint in TBI; lung shielding limits pulmonary dose to reduce the chance of fatal radiation pneumonitis.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-14 · _recall_
Electron beams are most appropriate for treating:

- **A.** Superficial targets such as skin lesions or the chest wall
- **B.** Deep-seated abdominal tumors
- **C.** Whole-pelvis nodal volumes at depth
- **D.** Intracranial deep metastases

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Electrons deposit dose over a limited depth with a rapid falloff beyond their range, making them ideal for superficial targets while sparing deeper tissue.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-15 · _recall_
For electron-beam therapy, the prescribed dose is conventionally normalized at:

- **A.** The surface (skin entrance)
- **B.** The depth of maximum dose (dmax)
- **C.** The 90% isodose only
- **D.** The exit surface

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Electron dose is typically specified at dmax (or a chosen percent-depth-dose line referenced to dmax), reflecting the buildup characteristics of the electron depth-dose curve.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-16 · _application_
A patient has a 4 mm-thick recurrent skin nodule on the forearm. Which beam type best treats the lesion while sparing underlying tissue?

- **A.** 18 MV photons
- **B.** Proton SOBP centered at 8 cm depth
- **C.** A low-energy electron beam
- **D.** Cobalt-60 photons

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A low-energy electron beam deposits dose superficially with a sharp distal falloff, treating the thin skin lesion while sparing tissue beneath it; bolus may be added to pull dmax to the surface.
**Source:** Khan

## Particle Therapy

### TRX-17 · _recall_
The characteristic depth-dose feature of a proton beam is the:

- **A.** Bragg peak, a sharp dose maximum near the end of range with minimal exit dose
- **B.** Exponential falloff identical to photons
- **C.** Broad surface maximum with a long tail
- **D.** Uniform dose at all depths

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Protons deposit most of their energy in the Bragg peak near the end of their range, with very little dose beyond it, allowing sparing of normal tissue distal to the target.
**Source:** IAEA handbook

### TRX-18 · _recall_
A spread-out Bragg peak (SOBP) is created in order to:

- **A.** Cover the full depth of a target by superimposing pristine peaks of varying energy
- **B.** Increase exit dose beyond the target
- **C.** Convert protons into electrons
- **D.** Eliminate the entrance dose entirely

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Because a single pristine Bragg peak is too narrow to cover most tumors, peaks of varying energy/range are summed to produce a uniform high-dose region (SOBP) across the target depth.
**Source:** IAEA handbook

### TRX-19 · _application_
A pediatric patient with a posterior fossa tumor is treated with protons partly to spare the cochlea and brainstem located just beyond the target. The dosimetric property most responsible for this advantage is:

- **A.** Higher entrance dose than photons
- **B.** Minimal dose distal to the Bragg peak / SOBP
- **C.** A broad penumbra
- **D.** Skin-sparing buildup

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Protons deliver little to no dose beyond the distal edge of the SOBP, reducing integral dose to normal structures behind the target—an advantage often emphasized in pediatric treatment.
**Source:** IAEA handbook

### TRX-20 · _recall_
Compared with a photon beam, a proton beam delivers:

- **A.** More exit dose beyond the target
- **B.** No entrance dose
- **C.** Dose only at the skin surface
- **D.** Less exit (distal) dose beyond the target

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A key clinical motivation for proton therapy is the sharply reduced dose deposited distal to the target, lowering integral dose to surrounding healthy tissue relative to photons.
**Source:** IAEA handbook

## Brachytherapy

**Explanation:** HDR afterloaders use a high-activity Iridium-192 source driven on a cable to dwell positions within applicators or catheters.

Traditional low-dose-rate (LDR) intracavitary gynecologic brachytherapy has commonly used which radionuclide?

- **D.** Palladium-103

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** LDR intracavitary gyn implants have traditionally used Cesium-137 tubes/sources loaded into the tandem and ovoids.

Permanent prostate brachytherapy seeds most commonly use which radionuclide?

- **A.** Cesium-137

### TRX-24 · _application_
A cervical-cancer patient is treated with a remote afterloader delivering a brief, high-activity Iridium-192 dwell sequence through a tandem and ovoids. This describes:

**Explanation:** A short, high-activity Iridium-192 treatment delivered by a remote afterloader is HDR intracavitary brachytherapy; LDR would instead use long-dwelling Cesium-137 sources.

### TRX-25 · _application_
A urology team implants permanent radioactive seeds throughout the prostate under ultrasound guidance; the seeds remain in place and decay over the following weeks. The correct radionuclide and modality are:

**Explanation:** Permanent prostate implants are interstitial and commonly use Iodine-125 seeds that stay in the gland permanently—distinct from the temporary Iridium-192 of HDR or Cesium-137 LDR intracavitary sources.

## Image Guidance & Record-and-Verify

### TRX-34 · _recall_
Megavoltage portal imaging at the treatment unit is primarily used to:

- **A.** Diagnose new tumors
- **B.** Verify field placement and patient setup relative to bony anatomy
- **C.** Measure absolute dose at depth
- **D.** Calibrate the linac output

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Portal (MV) images are acquired at the machine to confirm that the treatment field and patient alignment match the plan, typically by matching bony landmarks.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-35 · _recall_
A major advantage of cone-beam CT (CBCT) over planar portal imaging for daily setup is that CBCT provides:

- **A.** Only a single 2D projection
- **B.** Higher absolute dose accuracy than an ion chamber
- **C.** Volumetric soft-tissue and bony information for 3D registration
- **D.** Real-time MRI

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** CBCT acquires a volumetric dataset that allows 3D registration to the planning CT using soft tissue as well as bone, improving localization for sites where bony anatomy is a poor surrogate.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-36 · _application_
A prostate patient has implanted gold fiducial markers. At the treatment unit the therapist aligns to these markers rather than to the pelvic bones because:

- **A.** Bone moves more than the prostate
- **B.** Fiducials reduce the imaging dose to zero
- **C.** The prostate can shift relative to bone day-to-day, so fiducials better localize the actual target
- **D.** Markers eliminate the need for any imaging

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The prostate moves independently of the bony pelvis with bladder and rectal filling, so fiducial-based matching localizes the soft-tissue target more reliably than bony alignment.
**Source:** Khan

### TRX-37 · _application_
On CBCT for a lung SBRT patient, the therapist registers to the soft-tissue tumor seen in the image rather than to the spine. The rationale is that:

- **A.** The spine is invisible on CBCT
- **B.** Soft-tissue matching avoids all imaging dose
- **C.** Bony matching is never acceptable
- **D.** The tumor can move relative to the vertebral bodies, so soft-tissue matching localizes it better

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A peripheral lung lesion can move with respiration and lie off the bony anatomy, so matching the visualized soft-tissue target (or fiducials) is more accurate than a bone-only match.
**Source:** AAPM TG-101

### TRX-38 · _recall_
A record-and-verify (R&V) system's core function during treatment is to:

- **A.** Generate the treatment plan automatically
- **B.** Replace the medical physicist's calibration duties
- **C.** Acquire the simulation CT

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** R&V systems cross-check delivery parameters (energy, MU, gantry/collimator angles, field sizes, MLC, etc.) against the approved plan and halt or override treatment when a parameter is outside tolerance.

### TRX-39 · _application_
During setup, the R&V system halts the beam because the collimator angle entered differs from the plan by 5 degrees. The most appropriate therapist action is to:

**Explanation:** An R&V mismatch should be investigated and resolved so delivery matches the approved plan; overriding without verification risks a treatment error.

### TRX-40 · _recall_
Which is a primary safety benefit of an R&V system?

- **A.** It increases the prescribed dose automatically
- **B.** It removes the need for a physics chart check

## Machine Operation & Daily Setup

### TRX-41 · _recall_

### TRX-50 · _recall_

A thoracic patient midway through radiotherapy reports new odynophagia limiting solid intake. The therapist should:

- **A.** Discontinue all imaging
- **B.** Document and grade the esophagitis (CTCAE) and arrange supportive care such as analgesia and dietary modification
- **C.** Immediately increase the fraction size
- **D.** Reposition the lung blocks to cover the esophagus

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** New painful swallowing during thoracic treatment suggests acute esophagitis; appropriate management is CTCAE grading plus supportive measures (analgesics, dietary changes, hydration) with physician notification.
**Source:** NCI CTCAE v5
