# Patient Care — Patient & Medical Record Management

## Cancer Epidemiology & Risk Factors

### PMR-01 · recall
Which infectious agent is most strongly associated with the development of squamous cell carcinoma of the oropharynx and cervix?

- **A.** Hepatitis B virus (HBV)
- **B.** Human papillomavirus (HPV)
- **C.** Epstein–Barr virus (EBV)
- **D.** Hepatitis C virus (HCV)

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** High-risk HPV types (notably 16 and 18) are the principal infectious driver of cervical cancer and an increasing proportion of oropharyngeal cancers.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (HPV and Cancer)

### PMR-02 · recall
Chronic infection with hepatitis B and hepatitis C viruses most directly increases the risk of which malignancy?

- **A.** Pancreatic adenocarcinoma
- **B.** Renal cell carcinoma
- **C.** Hepatocellular carcinoma
- **D.** Gastric carcinoma

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Chronic HBV and HCV infection cause hepatic inflammation and cirrhosis, the dominant risk pathway for hepatocellular carcinoma.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Liver Cancer Prevention)

### PMR-03 · recall
Which occupational exposure is the classic cause of mesothelioma?

- **A.** Benzene
- **B.** Vinyl chloride
- **C.** Arsenic
- **D.** Asbestos

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Asbestos fiber inhalation is the established occupational cause of pleural and peritoneal mesothelioma, often with a long latency period.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Malignant Mesothelioma)

### PMR-04 · application
A patient who has smoked one pack per day for 30 years presents for treatment planning. Beyond lung cancer, the therapist should recognize that tobacco use also raises this patient's risk for cancers of the:

- **A.** Head/neck, esophagus, and bladder
- **B.** Thyroid and breast
- **C.** Bone and brain
- **D.** Skin and uterus

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Tobacco is a recognized carcinogen for a wide field of sites including the head and neck, esophagus, and urinary bladder, not just the lung.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Cigarette Smoking and Cancer)

### PMR-05 · application
A fair-skinned outdoor worker presents with a lesion. Which lifestyle/occupational factor is most relevant to counsel about for skin cancer risk?

- **A.** Dietary fat intake
- **B.** Caffeine consumption
- **C.** Sedentary behavior
- **D.** Cumulative ultraviolet (sun) exposure

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Cumulative and intermittent intense UV radiation exposure is the dominant modifiable risk factor for basal cell, squamous cell, and melanoma skin cancers.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Skin Cancer Prevention)

## TNM Staging & the Pathology Report

### PMR-06 · recall
In the TNM staging system, the "N" category describes:

- **A.** Regional lymph node involvement
- **B.** The number of metastatic organs involved
- **C.** The nuclear grade of the tumor
- **D.** The primary tumor's histologic type

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** T denotes the primary tumor extent, N denotes regional lymph node involvement, and M denotes distant metastasis.
**Source:** AJCC Cancer Staging Manual

### PMR-07 · recall
The prefix "p" in a stage designation such as pT2N1 indicates that the stage was determined by:

- **A.** Pretreatment imaging only
- **B.** Physician palpation alone
- **C.** Pathologic examination of resected tissue
- **D.** PET metabolic uptake

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The "p" prefix indicates pathologic staging based on histopathologic examination of surgically resected specimens, versus "c" for clinical staging.
**Source:** AJCC Cancer Staging Manual

### PMR-08 · application
The primary clinical purpose of assigning a TNM stage is to:

- **A.** Determine the exact monitor units for treatment
- **B.** Guide prognosis and standardize treatment selection
- **C.** Replace the pathology report
- **D.** Set the daily imaging schedule

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** TNM stage groups anatomic disease extent into categories that inform prognosis and guide evidence-based treatment selection and comparison.
**Source:** AJCC Cancer Staging Manual

### PMR-09 · recall
On a pathology report, a "positive margin" most directly means that:

- **A.** The tumor is high grade
- **B.** Tumor cells extend to the edge of the resected specimen
- **C.** Lymph nodes contain tumor
- **D.** Distant metastasis is present

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A positive surgical margin indicates tumor cells reach the inked cut edge of the specimen, suggesting residual disease may remain and influencing the need for further therapy.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Pathology Reports)

### PMR-10 · application
A pathology report notes "lymphovascular invasion present" and "grade 3." How should the therapist interpret these findings?

- **A.** Both are favorable, low-risk features
- **B.** Both are adverse features associated with more aggressive disease
- **C.** Grade 3 is favorable; LVI is irrelevant to RT
- **D.** They indicate the tumor was benign

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** High histologic grade and lymphovascular invasion are adverse prognostic features associated with greater aggressiveness and recurrence/metastasis risk.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Pathology Reports)

### PMR-11 · recall
For invasive breast cancer, which receptor panel is routinely reported and used to guide systemic and radiation management decisions?

- **A.** PSA, CEA, and AFP
- **B.** BUN, creatinine, and GFR
- **C.** CA-19-9 and CA-125
- **D.** ER, PR, and HER2

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR), and HER2 status are standard breast biomarkers guiding endocrine, anti-HER2, and treatment planning decisions.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Breast Cancer Treatment)

### PMR-12 · application
A breast pathology report reads "ER+/PR+/HER2-negative." Compared with a triple-negative tumor, this profile is generally associated with:

- **A.** A worse prognosis and no systemic options
- **B.** No effect on prognosis
- **C.** A more favorable prognosis and endocrine-therapy responsiveness
- **D.** Mandatory immediate metastasis workup only

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative breast cancers are generally responsive to endocrine therapy and carry a relatively more favorable prognosis than triple-negative disease.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Breast Cancer Treatment)

## Medical History, Imaging & Laboratory Review

### PMR-13 · application
When reviewing a patient's history of prior radiation therapy, the most important details to document for the current course are the:

- **A.** Patient's insurance and billing codes
- **B.** Name of the prior referring physician only
- **C.** Prior treatment site, total dose, and dates
- **D.** Patient's preferred appointment time

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Prior RT site, dose, and timing are essential to evaluate cumulative dose to overlapping tissues and organs at risk before re-irradiation.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Radiation Therapy

### PMR-14 · recall
A history of pregnancy or possible pregnancy is critical to establish before simulation and treatment primarily because of:

- **A.** Radiation risk to the developing fetus
- **B.** Scheduling preferences
- **C.** Contrast allergy concerns only
- **D.** Billing requirements

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Ionizing radiation poses risk to a developing fetus, so pregnancy status must be assessed and addressed before any radiographic simulation or treatment.
**Source:** IAEA Radiation Protection handbook

### PMR-15 · recall
Performance status scales such as the ECOG/Zubrod or Karnofsky index are used to document a patient's:

- **A.** Functional/activity level and ability to tolerate treatment
- **B.** Tumor grade
- **C.** Renal clearance
- **D.** Allergy profile

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Performance status quantifies a patient's functional capacity and is used to gauge prognosis and tolerance of aggressive therapy.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Performance Status)

### PMR-16 · application
During history review a patient reports prior cisplatin chemotherapy. The therapist should be especially attentive to which organ-function labs?

- **A.** Renal function (BUN/creatinine) and electrolytes
- **B.** Liver enzymes only
- **C.** Tumor markers only
- **D.** Coagulation studies only

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Cisplatin is nephrotoxic and can cause electrolyte wasting, so renal function and electrolytes warrant close attention in patients with this history.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Cisplatin)

### PMR-17 · recall
In radiation treatment planning, the CT simulation dataset is uniquely required because it provides:

- **A.** Electron-density information for dose calculation
- **B.** The best soft-tissue contrast available
- **C.** Metabolic activity of the tumor
- **D.** Real-time respiratory gating only

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** CT Hounsfield units convert to electron density, which the treatment planning system needs for accurate heterogeneity-corrected dose calculation.
**Source:** AAPM TG-66 (CT Simulation)

### PMR-18 · application
A brain tumor case requires precise delineation of the lesion against adjacent normal brain. Which imaging modality is most often fused with the planning CT for superior soft-tissue target definition?

- **A.** Plain radiography
- **B.** MRI
- **C.** Ultrasound
- **D.** Bone scan

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** MRI provides superior soft-tissue contrast for brain and many soft-tissue targets and is commonly co-registered with the planning CT for contouring.
**Source:** ICRU Report 83

### PMR-19 · recall
PET imaging contributes to staging and planning chiefly by demonstrating:

- **A.** Metabolic/functional activity of tissues
- **B.** Electron density for dose calculation
- **C.** Cortical bone detail
- **D.** Skin surface contour

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** FDG-PET reflects metabolic activity, helping detect active disease and involved nodes for staging and biologic target definition.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (PET Imaging)

### PMR-20 · application
A mobile lung tumor moves substantially with breathing. Which simulation approach best characterizes that motion for planning?

- **A.** 2D fluoroscopy single frame
- **B.** Standard 3D CT only
- **C.** Bone scan
- **D.** 4D (respiratory-correlated) CT simulation

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** 4D CT correlates image acquisition with the respiratory cycle to capture tumor motion and define an internal target volume.
**Source:** AAPM TG-76 (Respiratory Motion)

### PMR-21 · application
Before a patient begins treatment that may cause significant myelosuppression, which lab is most important to review for adequate blood counts?

- **A.** Liver function tests
- **B.** Serum PSA
- **C.** Urinalysis
- **D.** Complete blood count (CBC)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The CBC reports white cells, hemoglobin, and platelets, which must be monitored when treatment risks bone-marrow suppression.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Hematologic Toxicity)

### PMR-22 · recall
Which tumor marker is most commonly followed in prostate cancer?

- **A.** CEA
- **B.** AFP
- **C.** PSA
- **D.** CA-19-9

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Prostate-specific antigen (PSA) is the standard serum marker used in prostate cancer detection, risk stratification, and follow-up.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Prostate Cancer)

### PMR-23 · application
A patient with pancreatic cancer has a markedly elevated serum marker on the chart. Which marker is most characteristically associated with this disease?

- **A.** PSA
- **B.** CA-19-9
- **C.** AFP
- **D.** Beta-hCG

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** CA-19-9 is the marker most commonly associated with pancreatic and biliary cancers and is used to follow treatment response.
**Source:** NCI PDQ (Pancreatic Cancer)

## The Radiation Prescription & Dose Verification

### PMR-24 · recall
A complete radiation therapy prescription must include all of the following EXCEPT:

- **A.** Total dose and dose per fraction
- **B.** Treatment intent, modality/energy, and physician authorization
- **C.** Organ-at-risk dose constraints
- **D.** The patient's daily appointment preference

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A prescription specifies total dose, fractionation, intent, modality/energy, OAR constraints, and the authorizing physician's signature; appointment preference is scheduling, not a prescription element.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Radiation Therapy

### PMR-25 · recall
An independently calculated monitor-unit (or dose) check should typically agree with the treatment-planning-system value within:

- **A.** ±5% (with ≤3% preferred)
- **B.** ±15%
- **C.** ±25%
- **D.** ±0.1%

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Guidance recommends independent MU/dose agreement within about ±5%, with ≤3% preferred, before treatment proceeds.
**Source:** ICRU / AAPM TG-71

### PMR-26 · calculation
The treatment planning system prescribes 250 monitor units. The independent second check calculates 262 MU. What is the percent difference, and is it within the commonly used ±5% tolerance?

- **A.** About 12%, outside tolerance
- **B.** About 0.5%, within tolerance
- **C.** About 9.6%, outside tolerance
- **D.** About 4.8%, within tolerance

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** (262 − 250) / 250 = 12/250 = 4.8%, which falls within the ±5% agreement target (and would be flagged for review since it exceeds the preferred ≤3%).
**Source:** ICRU / AAPM TG-71

### PMR-27 · calculation
A course commonly delivers 2 Gy per fraction for 30 fractions. After the patient completes 18 fractions, what cumulative dose has been delivered?

- **A.** 24 Gy
- **B.** 60 Gy
- **C.** 18 Gy
- **D.** 36 Gy

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Cumulative dose = dose/fraction × fractions delivered = 2 Gy × 18 = 36 Gy.
**Source:** AAPM TG-71

## Treatment Records, EMR & Safety Reporting

### PMR-28 · recall
The daily treatment-delivery record should document all of the following EXCEPT:

- **A.** Date, fraction number, and monitor units
- **B.** The patient's home medication list in full detail
- **C.** Daily and cumulative dose and beam parameters
- **D.** Any treatment interruptions and therapist verification

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The daily record captures date, fraction number, MU, daily/cumulative dose, beam parameters, interruptions, and therapist verification; the complete home medication list belongs in the broader medical history rather than the daily delivery log.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Radiation Therapy

### PMR-29 · application
A therapist notices a near-miss in which a wrong setup photo was nearly used but caught before treatment. The most appropriate action is to:

- **A.** Ignore it because no dose was delivered
- **B.** Report it through the incident/near-miss reporting system
- **C.** Delete the record to avoid blame
- **D.** Wait until the end of the course to mention it

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Near-misses must be reported so that latent system weaknesses can be analyzed and corrected, improving safety even when no patient harm occurred.
**Source:** AAPM / IAEA Safety Reporting

### PMR-30 · application
As the team member who sees the patient daily, the radiation therapist's role as liaison most importantly includes:

- **A.** Independently changing the dose prescription as needed
- **B.** Interpreting the pathology report for the patient
- **C.** Recognizing and promptly communicating critical clinical findings to the physician/nurse
- **D.** Approving the physics chart check alone

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The therapist's daily contact makes them well positioned to detect changes (toxicity, pain, distress) and escalate critical findings to the care team, while prescription changes remain the physician's responsibility.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Radiation Therapy
