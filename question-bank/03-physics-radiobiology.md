# Safety — Radiation Physics & Radiobiology

_Original practice questions for ARRT Radiation Therapy registry review. Not actual exam items; not affiliated with the ARRT._

## Radioactive Sources & X-ray Production

### PHR-01 · _recall_
Which two gamma-ray energies are emitted by the decay of Cobalt-60?

- **A.** 0.51 and 1.02 MeV
- **B.** 0.66 and 1.25 MeV
- **C.** 1.17 and 1.33 MeV
- **D.** 2.50 and 5.27 MeV

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Co-60 emits two gamma photons per disintegration at 1.17 MeV and 1.33 MeV, giving an average of about 1.25 MeV.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-02 · _recall_
The physical half-life of Cobalt-60 is approximately:

- **A.** 5.27 years
- **B.** 5.27 days
- **C.** 5.27 months
- **D.** 30 years

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Co-60 has a half-life of 5.27 years, requiring periodic source replacement in teletherapy units as output decays about 1% per month.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-03 · _recall_
Bremsstrahlung (braking) radiation is produced when:

- **A.** An inner-shell electron vacancy is filled by an outer-shell electron
- **B.** A photon ejects an orbital electron from the K-shell
- **C.** A high-speed electron is decelerated and deflected by the nucleus of a target atom
- **D.** A positron and electron annihilate

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Bremsstrahlung is the continuous x-ray spectrum created when accelerated electrons are decelerated/deflected in the strong field of a high-Z nucleus, converting kinetic energy to photons.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-04 · _recall_
Characteristic x-rays are emitted when:

- **A.** An electron is decelerated near a nucleus
- **B.** A nucleus undergoes beta decay
- **C.** Two photons combine into one
- **D.** An inner-shell electron vacancy is filled by an electron from a higher shell

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Characteristic radiation has discrete energies equal to the difference in binding energies between the shells as an outer electron drops to fill an inner-shell vacancy.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-05 · _application_
A linac target is bombarded by a higher-energy electron beam. Compared with a lower-energy beam, the bremsstrahlung x-rays produced are:

- **A.** Lower in average energy and more penetrating
- **B.** Identical because target material sets the energy
- **C.** Higher in average energy and more forward-peaked
- **D.** Purely characteristic in spectrum

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Higher incident electron energy raises the maximum and average photon energy of the bremsstrahlung spectrum and makes the emission more forward-directed (peaked toward 0°).
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-06 · _recall_
Which becquerel/curie equivalence is correct?

- **A.** 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10^7 Bq
- **B.** 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10^10 Bq
- **C.** 1 Bq = 3.7 × 10^10 Ci
- **D.** 1 Ci = 100 Bq

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The becquerel is one disintegration per second; the curie equals 3.7 × 10^10 Bq (disintegrations per second).
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

## Photon Interactions

### PHR-07 · _recall_
The photoelectric effect probability per atom is approximately proportional to:

- **A.** Z
- **B.** Z^2
- **C.** Z^3
- **D.** Independent of Z

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Photoelectric absorption varies roughly as Z^3 (and falls steeply with energy, ~1/E^3), which is why bone and contrast media are conspicuous at diagnostic (low) energies.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-08 · _recall_
Which photon interaction dominates in soft tissue at megavoltage therapy energies?

- **A.** Photoelectric effect
- **B.** Pair production
- **C.** Compton scattering
- **D.** Coherent (Rayleigh) scattering

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** In the therapeutic MV range, Compton scattering is the dominant interaction in tissue, and because it is nearly independent of Z, dose is roughly proportional to electron density.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-09 · _recall_
The threshold photon energy required for pair production is:

- **A.** 1.02 MeV
- **B.** 0.511 MeV
- **C.** 1.25 MeV
- **D.** 2.04 MeV

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Pair production requires at least 1.02 MeV — the energy needed to create an electron–positron pair (2 × 0.511 MeV rest mass) in the nuclear field.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-10 · _application_
A 6 MV beam interacts in muscle and a 60 kVp beam interacts in bone. Which interactions predominate, respectively?

- **A.** Photoelectric; Compton
- **B.** Pair production; Compton
- **C.** Compton; photoelectric
- **D.** Compton; pair production

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Megavoltage beams in tissue are dominated by Compton scattering; low-kV beams in high-Z bone are dominated by the photoelectric effect (∝ Z^3).
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-11 · _application_
Why does a megavoltage photon beam deliver similar dose to bone and soft tissue, whereas a kilovoltage beam deposits much more dose in bone?

- **A.** Pair production preferentially spares bone
- **B.** Bone has lower electron density than soft tissue at MV energies
- **C.** Characteristic x-rays from calcium dominate at MV energies
- **D.** Compton scattering is nearly Z-independent, so MV dose tracks electron density rather than Z

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** At MV energies Compton interactions dominate and depend on electron density (nearly Z-independent), so bone and tissue absorb comparably; at kV energies the photoelectric Z^3 dependence greatly enhances bone dose.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-12 · _recall_
After a positron from pair production annihilates with an electron, the result is:

- **A.** A single 1.02 MeV photon
- **B.** A characteristic x-ray
- **C.** Two 0.511 MeV photons emitted ~180° apart
- **D.** A neutron

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Positron annihilation produces two 0.511 MeV (annihilation) photons traveling in nearly opposite directions to conserve momentum.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

## Attenuation, Beam Quality & HVL

### PHR-13 · _recall_
The half-value layer (HVL) of a beam is the thickness of absorber that:

- **A.** Stops all photons
- **B.** Reduces beam intensity to one-half its initial value
- **C.** Doubles the beam intensity
- **D.** Reduces intensity to one-tenth

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** HVL is the absorber thickness that attenuates beam intensity to 50% and is a practical index of beam quality (penetrating ability).
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398 / dosimetry)

### PHR-14 · _calculation_
A narrow beam has an HVL of 2 mm. After passing through 6 mm of that absorber, what fraction of the original intensity remains?

- **A.** 1/2
- **B.** 1/4
- **C.** 1/16
- **D.** 1/8

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** 6 mm = 3 HVLs, so the transmitted fraction is (1/2)^3 = 1/8.
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398)

### PHR-15 · _calculation_
A beam has an HVL of 3 mm Al. How many HVLs are needed to reduce the intensity to less than 10% of the original, and what thickness is that?

- **A.** 2 HVLs (6 mm)
- **B.** 4 HVLs (12 mm)
- **C.** 3 HVLs (9 mm)
- **D.** 7 HVLs (21 mm)

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** (1/2)^3 = 0.125 (12.5%, still >10%); (1/2)^4 = 0.0625 (6.25%, <10%). So 4 HVLs = 4 × 3 mm = 12 mm.
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398)

### PHR-16 · _calculation_
Using exponential attenuation I = I₀e^(−μx), if the linear attenuation coefficient μ = 0.10 mm⁻¹, what is the transmitted fraction through 10 mm? (Use e⁻¹ ≈ 0.37.)

- **A.** ~0.90
- **B.** ~0.50
- **C.** ~0.37
- **D.** ~0.10

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** μx = 0.10 × 10 = 1.0, so I/I₀ = e^(−1.0) ≈ 0.37.
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398)

### PHR-17 · _calculation_
The linear attenuation coefficient and HVL are related by HVL = 0.693/μ. If μ = 0.231 cm⁻¹, the HVL is:

- **A.** 0.30 cm
- **B.** 1.5 cm
- **C.** 3.0 cm
- **D.** 6.9 cm

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** HVL = 0.693/0.231 = 3.0 cm (0.693 = ln 2).
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398)

### PHR-18 · _application_
Two beams have HVL₁ = 1 mm Cu and HVL₂ = 3 mm Cu. Which statement is correct?

- **A.** Beam 1 is more penetrating (higher quality)
- **B.** They have identical penetrating power
- **C.** HVL is unrelated to penetration
- **D.** Beam 2 is more penetrating (higher quality)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A larger HVL means more material is needed to halve the beam, indicating a more penetrating, higher-quality beam.
**Source:** IAEA handbook (TRS-398)

### PHR-19 · _application_
Adding a small amount of filtration to a kilovoltage x-ray beam will generally:

- **A.** Decrease the HVL and soften the beam
- **B.** Leave the HVL unchanged
- **C.** Increase the HVL and harden the beam
- **D.** Convert the beam to monoenergetic

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Filtration preferentially removes low-energy photons, raising the mean energy ("beam hardening") and increasing the HVL.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

## Inverse-Square Law & Calibration

### PHR-20 · _recall_
The inverse-square law for radiation intensity from a point source is expressed as:

- **A.** D₂ = D₁ × (d₁/d₂)²
- **B.** D₂ = D₁ × (d₂/d₁)²
- **C.** D₂ = D₁ × (d₁/d₂)
- **D.** D₂ = D₁ × (d₂/d₁)

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Intensity falls with the square of distance, so D₂ = D₁ × (d₁/d₂)².
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-21 · _calculation_
The dose rate is 200 cGy/min at 100 cm from a source. Using inverse-square only, what is the dose rate at 200 cm?

- **A.** 400 cGy/min
- **B.** 100 cGy/min
- **C.** 25 cGy/min
- **D.** 50 cGy/min

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** D₂ = 200 × (100/200)² = 200 × 0.25 = 50 cGy/min.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-22 · _calculation_
A reading of 80 cGy/min is measured at 80 cm. By inverse-square, what is the dose rate at 40 cm?

- **A.** 40 cGy/min
- **B.** 160 cGy/min
- **C.** 20 cGy/min
- **D.** 320 cGy/min

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** D₂ = 80 × (80/40)² = 80 × 4 = 320 cGy/min.
**Source:** Khan, *The Physics of Radiation Therapy*

### PHR-23 · _calculation_
At 1 m the exposure rate is 36 R/h. At what distance would the rate drop to 4 R/h by inverse-square?

- **A.** 3 m
- **B.** 2 m
- **C.** 6 m
- **D.** 9 m

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** (d₁/d₂)² = 4/36 = 1/9, so d₂ = d₁ × 3 = 3 m. (Ratio of rates equals inverse square of distance ratio.)
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-24 · _recall_
In the AAPM TG-51 protocol, the reference depth for calibrating a megavoltage **photon** beam in water is:

- **A.** 10 cm
- **B.** dmax
- **C.** 5 cm
- **D.** 15 cm

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** TG-51 specifies absorbed-dose-to-water calibration of photon beams at a reference depth of 10 cm in water.
**Source:** AAPM TG-51

### PHR-25 · _recall_
In TG-51, the beam-quality specifier used for **electron** beams is:

- **A.** %dd(10)x
- **B.** HVL in Al
- **C.** The nominal energy in MeV
- **D.** R50 (the depth of 50% dose)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Electron beam quality in TG-51 is specified by R50, the depth in water at which dose falls to 50% of maximum.
**Source:** AAPM TG-51

### PHR-26 · _application_
TG-51 calibration is performed with the ionization chamber positioned in:

- **A.** Air at the source
- **B.** A solid lead block
- **C.** A water phantom at the reference depth
- **D.** Vacuum

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** TG-51 uses a calibrated ion chamber in a water phantom at the reference depth to determine absorbed dose to water under reference conditions.
**Source:** AAPM TG-51

## Units & Dosimetric Quantities

### PHR-27 · _recall_
The gray (Gy) is the SI unit of:

- **A.** Activity
- **B.** Equivalent dose
- **C.** Absorbed dose
- **D.** Exposure

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The gray is absorbed dose, defined as one joule of energy absorbed per kilogram of matter (1 Gy = 1 J/kg).
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-28 · _recall_
The sievert (Sv) is the SI unit of:

- **A.** Absorbed dose
- **B.** Activity
- **C.** Kerma
- **D.** Equivalent (and effective) dose

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The sievert expresses equivalent/effective dose — absorbed dose weighted for radiation type and tissue sensitivity — for radiation-protection purposes.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-29 · _calculation_
A patient receives 2 Gy per fraction. How many rad is that?

- **A.** 20 rad
- **B.** 200 rad
- **C.** 2 rad
- **D.** 2000 rad

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** 1 Gy = 100 rad, so 2 Gy × 100 = 200 rad.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-30 · _calculation_
An occupational equivalent dose of 50 mSv corresponds to how many rem?

- **A.** 0.5 rem
- **B.** 5 rem
- **C.** 50 rem
- **D.** 500 rem

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** 1 Sv = 100 rem, so 50 mSv = 0.05 Sv × 100 = 5 rem.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-31 · _calculation_
A sealed source has an activity of 10 mCi. Express this in becquerels.

- **A.** 3.7 × 10^7 Bq
- **B.** 3.7 × 10^9 Bq
- **C.** 3.7 × 10^10 Bq
- **D.** 3.7 × 10^8 Bq

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** 1 Ci = 3.7 × 10^10 Bq, so 10 mCi = 0.010 Ci × 3.7 × 10^10 = 3.7 × 10^8 Bq.
**Source:** NRC 10 CFR 20

### PHR-32 · _application_
For low-LET radiation such as therapeutic photons and electrons, the numerical value of equivalent dose (Sv) relative to absorbed dose (Gy) is:

- **A.** Always 20 times larger
- **B.** Equal, because the radiation weighting factor is 1
- **C.** Always smaller by a factor of 100
- **D.** Unrelated to absorbed dose

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Photons and electrons have a radiation weighting factor (w_R) of 1, so 1 Gy of absorbed dose equals 1 Sv of equivalent dose.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

## Radiobiology: Cell Survival, LET & the Four R's

### PHR-33 · _recall_
In the linear-quadratic model, cell survival is described by:

- **A.** S = e^(−αD − βD²)
- **B.** S = e^(αD + βD²)
- **C.** S = αD + βD²
- **D.** S = e^(−μD)

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** The LQ model gives surviving fraction S = e^(−αD − βD²), where αD represents lethal (single-track) damage and βD² represents sublethal (two-track) damage.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-34 · _recall_
Typical α/β values are approximately:

- **A.** 3 Gy for tumors/early effects and 10 Gy for late effects
- **B.** 1 Gy for both
- **C.** 100 Gy for tumors and 50 Gy for late effects
- **D.** 10 Gy for tumors/early effects and 3 Gy for late effects

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Tumors and acutely responding (early) tissues typically have a high α/β (~10 Gy); late-responding normal tissues have a low α/β (~3 Gy), making them more sensitive to large fraction sizes.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-35 · _recall_
The "Four R's" of radiobiology that explain the rationale for fractionation are:

- **A.** Recovery, Resistance, Replication, Regression
- **B.** Repair, Reflection, Refraction, Radiation
- **C.** Repair, Repopulation, Reoxygenation, Reassortment
- **D.** Reoxygenation, Recombination, Relaxation, Resorption

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The Four R's are Repair (of sublethal damage), Repopulation, Reoxygenation, and Reassortment (redistribution within the cell cycle).
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-36 · _application_
Which of the Four R's most directly explains why hypoxic tumor regions become more radiosensitive over a fractionated course?

- **A.** Repair
- **B.** Repopulation
- **C.** Reoxygenation
- **D.** Reassortment

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Between fractions, surviving well-oxygenated cells are killed and previously hypoxic cells gain access to oxygen (reoxygenation), increasing their radiosensitivity for subsequent fractions.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-37 · _recall_
The oxygen enhancement ratio (OER) for low-LET radiation is approximately:

- **A.** 1.0
- **B.** 1.5
- **C.** 10
- **D.** 2.5–3.0

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The OER for x-rays/gamma rays is about 2.5–3.0, meaning hypoxic cells require roughly 2.5–3 times the dose of oxygenated cells for equal killing.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-38 · _application_
As LET increases (e.g., from photons toward alpha particles/heavy ions), the OER:

- **A.** Increases toward 5
- **B.** Remains constant at ~3
- **C.** Decreases toward 1
- **D.** Becomes negative

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** High-LET radiation produces dense, direct, largely oxygen-independent damage, so the OER decreases toward 1 (the oxygen effect diminishes).
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-39 · _application_
Relative biological effectiveness (RBE) compares a test radiation to a reference (typically 250 kVp x-rays). High-LET radiation generally has an RBE that is:

- **A.** Less than 1
- **B.** Equal to 1
- **C.** Zero
- **D.** Greater than 1

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** High-LET radiation deposits energy more densely and is biologically more effective per unit dose, giving RBE > 1 relative to low-LET reference radiation.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-40 · _recall_
The majority of cell killing by low-LET radiation (photons/electrons) occurs through:

- **A.** Direct action on DNA only
- **B.** Indirect action via free radicals (e.g., hydroxyl radicals from water)
- **C.** Thermal heating of the cell
- **D.** Characteristic x-ray emission inside the nucleus

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** For low-LET radiation, ~2/3 of DNA damage is indirect — radiolysis of water produces reactive free radicals (notably •OH) that damage DNA; direct ionization of DNA is the minority pathway.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

## Fractionation Math, Deterministic vs Stochastic & Tolerances

### PHR-41 · _calculation_
For a tumor with α/β = 10 Gy, calculate the BED of 30 fractions of 2 Gy using BED = n·d·(1 + d/(α/β)).

- **A.** 72 Gy
- **B.** 60 Gy
- **C.** 66 Gy
- **D.** 120 Gy

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** BED = 30 × 2 × (1 + 2/10) = 60 × 1.2 = 72 Gy₁₀.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-42 · _calculation_
For late-responding tissue with α/β = 3 Gy, find the BED of a single 8 Gy fraction (BED = n·d·(1 + d/(α/β))).

- **A.** 8.0 Gy
- **B.** 21.3 Gy
- **C.** 64 Gy
- **D.** 29.3 Gy

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** BED = 1 × 8 × (1 + 8/3) = 8 × 3.667 = 29.3 Gy₃.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-43 · _calculation_
A tumor regimen has BED = 72 Gy with α/β = 10 Gy. Convert to EQD2 using EQD2 = BED / (1 + 2/(α/β)).

- **A.** 50 Gy
- **B.** 66 Gy
- **C.** 72 Gy
- **D.** 60 Gy

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** EQD2 = 72 / (1 + 2/10) = 72 / 1.2 = 60 Gy.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-44 · _calculation_
Using BED = n·d·(1 + d/(α/β)) with α/β = 10 Gy, which regimen delivers the higher tumor BED: (X) 60 Gy in 30 fractions of 2 Gy, or (Y) 50 Gy in 20 fractions of 2.5 Gy?

- **A.** Y (62.5 Gy) is higher than X (72 Gy)
- **B.** X (72 Gy) is higher than Y (62.5 Gy)
- **C.** Both equal at 60 Gy
- **D.** Cannot be determined

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** X: 60 × (1 + 2/10) = 72 Gy₁₀. Y: 50 × (1 + 2.5/10) = 50 × 1.25 = 62.5 Gy₁₀. So X > Y.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-45 · _calculation_
Late-tissue tolerance is being compared. With α/β = 3 Gy, what is the EQD2 of a 5-fraction SBRT course of 10 Gy/fraction (50 Gy total)? (BED = n·d·(1 + d/(α/β)); EQD2 = BED/(1 + 2/(α/β)).)

- **A.** 50 Gy
- **B.** 100 Gy
- **C.** 130 Gy
- **D.** 217 Gy

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** BED = 5 × 10 × (1 + 10/3) = 50 × 4.333 = 216.7 Gy₃; EQD2 = 216.7 / (1 + 2/3) = 216.7 / 1.667 ≈ 130 Gy.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-46 · _recall_
A deterministic (tissue-reaction) effect of radiation is characterized by:

- **A.** No threshold; probability rises with dose
- **B.** A fixed severity regardless of dose
- **C.** A threshold dose, with severity increasing as dose increases above it
- **D.** Occurrence only at occupational dose limits

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Deterministic effects (e.g., cataracts, skin erythema, fibrosis) have a practical threshold; once exceeded, the severity of the effect increases with dose.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-47 · _recall_
A stochastic effect of radiation (e.g., carcinogenesis) is best described as:

- **A.** Having a clear threshold dose
- **B.** Having no threshold, with probability (not severity) increasing with dose
- **C.** Increasing in severity with dose
- **D.** Occurring only above 0.5 Gy

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Stochastic effects are assumed to have no threshold; increasing dose raises the probability of occurrence, while the severity of the resulting disease is independent of dose.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-48 · _application_
Cataract formation in the lens of the eye is an example of which type of effect, and its approximate threshold is:

- **A.** Stochastic; no threshold
- **B.** Deterministic; ~0.5 Gy threshold
- **C.** Deterministic; ~45 Gy threshold
- **D.** Stochastic; ~3 Gy threshold

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Cataract is a deterministic (tissue-reaction) effect; the lens is among the most radiosensitive structures, with a threshold on the order of 0.5 Gy for detectable opacities.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-49 · _recall_
The conventional TD5/5 tolerance dose for the spinal cord is approximately:

- **A.** 10–15 Gy
- **B.** 45–50 Gy
- **C.** 20–25 Gy
- **D.** 70–75 Gy

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The spinal cord TD5/5 (5% complication risk at 5 years) is conventionally cited around 45–50 Gy with standard fractionation, making it a key dose-limiting structure.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*

### PHR-50 · _application_
For whole-lung irradiation, a commonly used planning constraint to limit radiation pneumonitis is a mean lung dose of approximately:

- **A.** ≤ 20 Gy
- **B.** ≤ 5 Gy
- **C.** ≤ 45 Gy
- **D.** ≤ 60 Gy

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Keeping the mean lung dose at or below roughly 20 Gy (with attention to V20) is a widely used constraint to reduce the risk of radiation pneumonitis.
**Source:** Hall & Giaccia, *Radiobiology for the Radiologist*
