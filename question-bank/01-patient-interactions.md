# Patient Care — Patient Interactions & Management
_Original practice questions for ARRT Radiation Therapy registry review. Not actual exam items; not affiliated with the ARRT._

## Communication, Health Literacy & History

### PIM-01 · _application_
A patient scheduled for daily pelvic treatment reads at a basic level and appears confused by the printed instruction sheet. Which approach best supports health literacy?

- **A.** Hand the patient a more detailed brochure to read at home
- **B.** Ask a co-worker to repeat the same written instructions louder
- **C.** Document that the patient is noncompliant and proceed
- **D.** Use plain language, demonstrate the step, and have the patient teach it back

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The teach-back method confirms understanding using plain language and demonstration, which is the recommended strategy for low health literacy. Repeating or adding written material does not address comprehension.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Medical Imaging and Radiation Therapy

### PIM-02 · _recall_
When using the teach-back technique to verify patient understanding, the therapist asks the patient to do which of the following?

- **A.** Sign a statement confirming they understood
- **B.** Read the instruction sheet aloud verbatim
- **C.** Restate the information in their own words
- **D.** Rate their understanding on a 1-to-10 scale

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Teach-back asks the patient to restate information in their own words so the educator can confirm and correct understanding. A signature or self-rating does not demonstrate actual comprehension.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Medical Imaging and Radiation Therapy

### PIM-03 · _application_
Before the first treatment, a therapist reviews the chart and notes the patient has an implanted cardiac pacemaker. What is the most appropriate initial action?

- **A.** Verify that the device location and management plan have been addressed by the care team before proceeding
- **B.** Cancel all treatment until the device is removed
- **C.** Ignore it because pacemakers are unaffected by radiation
- **D.** Increase the daily dose to compensate for device shielding

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Reviewing pertinent history includes confirming that implanted devices have a documented management plan, since radiation can affect cardiac implantable electronic devices. The therapist confirms the plan rather than removing the device or ignoring it.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Medical Imaging and Radiation Therapy

### PIM-04 · _recall_
Reviewing a patient's pertinent history before treatment primarily serves to do which of the following?

- **A.** Identify factors that affect safety, positioning, and the treatment plan
- **B.** Determine the patient's insurance coverage
- **C.** Replace the physician's clinical evaluation
- **D.** Establish the billing code for the visit

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** History review identifies allergies, devices, mobility limits, prior radiation, and other factors that influence safe care delivery. It supplements, but does not replace, the physician's evaluation.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Medical Imaging and Radiation Therapy

### PIM-05 · _application_
During CT simulation, a patient who must hold a deep breath repeatedly releases early. Which coaching strategy is most effective?

- **A.** Tell the patient to simply try harder without further explanation
- **B.** Restrain the chest with additional straps
- **C.** Rehearse the breath-hold before scanning and give clear, calm verbal cues during acquisition
- **D.** Proceed with free breathing and document patient refusal

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Rehearsing the breath-hold and providing consistent verbal cues improves compliance and reproducibility during CT simulation. Restraint or vague encouragement does not build the needed skill.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards for Medical Imaging and Radiation Therapy

### PIM-06 · _application_
A non-English-speaking patient arrives for simulation. Which is the most appropriate way to obtain accurate communication?

- **A.** Use the patient's adult child to interpret medical details
- **B.** Speak slowly in English and use gestures
- **C.** Postpone care until the patient learns English
- **D.** Use a qualified medical interpreter (in person or via phone/video)

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** A qualified medical interpreter ensures accurate, confidential, and unbiased communication. Family members may misinterpret terms or filter sensitive information and should not serve as primary interpreters.
**Source:** HHS Office for Civil Rights / ASRT Practice Standards

## Cultural Competence, Education & Informed Consent

### PIM-07 · _recall_
In the radiation oncology setting, who is responsible for obtaining a patient's informed consent for treatment?

- **A.** The treating physician
- **B.** The radiation therapist
- **C.** The dosimetrist
- **D.** The front-desk scheduler

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Informed consent is obtained by the physician, who discusses risks, benefits, and alternatives. The therapist may reinforce information, verify consent is on file, and document the interaction.
**Source:** ACR-ASTRO Practice Parameter / ARRT Standards of Ethics

### PIM-08 · _application_
A patient about to begin treatment says, "I'm not sure what the doctor meant by side effects." What is the therapist's most appropriate role?

- **A.** Provide the full risk-benefit discussion and obtain a new signature
- **B.** Tell the patient not to worry about it
- **C.** Reinforce general education and notify the physician to clarify before treatment proceeds
- **D.** Cancel the entire course of treatment

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The therapist reinforces education within scope but must refer unanswered consent questions back to the physician. The therapist does not perform the formal consent discussion.
**Source:** ACR-ASTRO Practice Parameter / ARRT Standards of Ethics

### PIM-09 · _recall_
Informed consent documentation generally must include which of the following elements?

- **A.** The patient's insurance policy number
- **B.** The therapist's home contact information
- **C.** Risks, benefits, alternatives, and the patient's voluntary agreement
- **D.** A guarantee of cure

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Valid informed consent documents that risks, benefits, and alternatives were disclosed and that the patient agreed voluntarily and with capacity. A cure can never be guaranteed.
**Source:** ACR-ASTRO Practice Parameter

### PIM-10 · _application_
A patient declines to remove a religious head covering for a head-and-neck setup that does not require its removal. The best response is to:

- **A.** Insist on removal to standardize the setup
- **B.** Refuse to treat the patient
- **C.** Remove it without asking once the patient is positioned
- **D.** Accommodate the covering when it does not interfere with treatment delivery or safety

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Culturally competent care accommodates personal and religious practices when they do not compromise safety or treatment accuracy. Forced removal violates patient dignity and autonomy.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / ARRT Standards of Ethics

### PIM-11 · _recall_
Cultural competence in patient care is best described as:

- **A.** Respecting and responding effectively to patients' diverse values, beliefs, and needs
- **B.** Treating every patient in exactly the same way regardless of background
- **C.** Avoiding any discussion of culture with patients
- **D.** Assigning patients to providers of the same ethnicity

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Cultural competence means recognizing and respectfully responding to diverse beliefs and needs, not applying identical treatment to all. Identical treatment ignores individual differences.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards

### PIM-12 · _application_
When working with a professional interpreter, the therapist should direct their speech and eye contact toward:

- **A.** The interpreter, since they relay the message
- **B.** The patient, speaking in the first person
- **C.** The computer screen to maintain efficiency
- **D.** Whoever is closest to the door

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** The therapist speaks directly to the patient in the first person while the interpreter conveys the message, preserving the therapeutic relationship. Addressing the interpreter sidelines the patient.
**Source:** HHS Office for Civil Rights / ASRT Practice Standards

## Psychosocial Support & Special Populations

### PIM-13 · _application_
During treatment a patient states, "I don't see the point of going on anymore." What is the therapist's most appropriate action?

- **A.** Reassure the patient it is just stress and continue treatment
- **B.** Tell the patient to discuss it at the next monthly visit
- **C.** Take the statement seriously and escalate to the physician/care team for mental-health assessment per protocol
- **D.** Ignore the comment to avoid embarrassing the patient

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Statements suggesting suicidal ideation require immediate escalation to the care team for assessment per institutional protocol. Delaying or dismissing the comment endangers the patient.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / institutional crisis-escalation policy

### PIM-14 · _recall_
The distress screening tool commonly recommended in oncology to identify patients needing psychosocial support uses a:

- **A.** Glasgow Coma Scale
- **B.** Body mass index chart
- **C.** 0-to-10 distress thermometer
- **D.** Visual acuity chart

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** The NCCN distress thermometer is a 0-to-10 self-report scale used to screen oncology patients for distress. The other tools assess unrelated parameters.
**Source:** NCCN Distress Management / ASRT Practice Standards

### PIM-15 · _application_
A 4-year-old is scheduled for daily treatment requiring stillness in a mask. Which intervention best supports compliance without sedation when possible?

- **A.** Tell the child to act like an adult
- **B.** Withhold all explanation to avoid scaring the child
- **C.** Require the parent to leave the building during simulation
- **D.** Involve a child-life specialist and use play-based, developmentally appropriate preparation

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Child-life specialists and play-based preparation help young children cooperate and reduce the need for sedation. Developmentally inappropriate demands or withholding information increase fear and movement.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / child-life best practice

### PIM-16 · _recall_
For a pediatric patient, consent for treatment is generally provided by:

- **A.** The child, who has full legal capacity
- **B.** A parent or legal guardian acting as surrogate decision-maker
- **C.** The radiation therapist
- **D.** The hospital billing office

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Minors generally lack legal capacity to consent, so a parent or legal guardian serves as surrogate; the child's assent may also be sought. The therapist does not provide consent.
**Source:** ACR-ASTRO Practice Parameter / ARRT Standards of Ethics

### PIM-17 · _recall_
Frailty screening tools used in geriatric oncology include which of the following?

- **A.** APGAR, Apgar, and Bishop scores
- **B.** Glasgow, Wells, and CHA2DS2-VASc scores
- **C.** Karnofsky color scale only
- **D.** G8, VES-13, and the Edmonton Frail Scale

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** The G8, VES-13, and Edmonton Frail Scale are validated geriatric frailty/vulnerability screening instruments. The other scores assess obstetric, neurologic, or thrombotic risk.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / geriatric oncology assessment

### PIM-18 · _application_
An elderly patient with an unsteady gait and recent falls arrives for treatment. The most appropriate safety measure is to:

- **A.** Allow the patient to walk to the table unassisted to promote independence
- **B.** Implement fall-precaution measures, assist transfers, and clear the path of hazards
- **C.** Place the patient in restraints for the entire visit
- **D.** Reschedule until the patient no longer falls

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Identified fall risk warrants fall precautions, assisted transfers, and a clear environment. Restraints are inappropriate, and unassisted ambulation increases injury risk.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / fall-prevention guidance

### PIM-19 · _application_
When communicating with a geriatric patient who has age-related hearing loss, the therapist should:

- **A.** Shout from across the room
- **B.** Communicate only in writing
- **C.** Face the patient, speak clearly at a moderate pace, and reduce background noise
- **D.** Speak rapidly to save time

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Facing the patient, speaking clearly, and reducing background noise improves comprehension for hearing-impaired patients. Shouting distorts speech and can be perceived as disrespectful.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards

## Vital Signs & Acute Symptom Management

### PIM-20 · _recall_
Which set best represents normal resting adult vital sign ranges?

- **A.** HR 60–100 bpm, RR 12–20/min, BP ~120/80 mmHg, temp ~98.6°F (37°C)
- **B.** HR 100–140 bpm, RR 25–35/min, BP ~80/40 mmHg, temp ~104°F
- **C.** HR 30–50 bpm, RR 4–8/min, BP ~200/120 mmHg, temp ~95°F
- **D.** HR 80–160 bpm, RR 40–60/min, BP ~70/30 mmHg, temp ~100°F

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Normal adult ranges are roughly HR 60–100 bpm, RR 12–20/min, BP about 120/80 mmHg, and temperature near 37°C (98.6°F). The other options describe abnormal values.
**Source:** AHA / Washington & Leaver, Principles and Practice of Radiation Therapy

### PIM-21 · _recall_
An adult blood pressure reading of 150/95 mmHg is best classified as:

- **A.** Normal
- **B.** Hypotension
- **C.** Hypertension (elevated/high)
- **D.** Normal for athletes only

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** A reading of 150/95 mmHg exceeds the normal threshold and indicates hypertension. It is neither normal nor hypotensive.
**Source:** AHA blood pressure categories

### PIM-22 · _application_
A patient on the treatment table reports nausea and then vomits. The therapist's first priority is to:

- **A.** Protect the airway by turning the patient to the side and managing aspiration risk
- **B.** Continue treatment to avoid delays
- **C.** Give the patient water to drink immediately
- **D.** Document the event after the patient leaves

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** The immediate priority during vomiting is airway protection and aspiration prevention by positioning the patient on their side. Treatment is paused and the event is then documented and reported.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / institutional protocol

### PIM-23 · _application_
A patient becomes lightheaded and pale while standing after treatment and is about to faint. The most appropriate action is to:

- **A.** Have them keep walking to "shake it off"
- **B.** Lower them safely to a seated or supine position and elevate the legs while monitoring
- **C.** Offer caffeine immediately
- **D.** Leave them alone to recover in privacy

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Syncope (or pre-syncope) is managed by safely lowering the patient and elevating the legs to restore cerebral perfusion while monitoring vitals. Continued ambulation risks a fall and injury.
**Source:** AHA BLS / Washington & Leaver

### PIM-24 · _application_
A patient reports increasing dyspnea during setup. After ensuring positioning is not the cause, the therapist should:

- **A.** Ignore it because anxiety is common
- **B.** Assess the patient, provide reassurance, and notify the physician/nurse; provide oxygen if ordered
- **C.** Immediately administer a beta-blocker
- **D.** Speed up treatment to finish faster

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** New or worsening dyspnea warrants assessment, reassurance, and escalation to the physician/nurse, with oxygen given only per order. Therapists do not independently administer cardiac medications.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / ASRT scope of practice

### PIM-25 · _recall_
Which symptom is an example of an ACUTE radiation side effect rather than a late effect?

- **A.** Fibrosis
- **B.** Moist desquamation of the skin during a treatment course
- **C.** Telangiectasia developing years later
- **D.** Secondary malignancy

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Acute effects such as erythema and moist desquamation appear during or shortly after the treatment course. Fibrosis, telangiectasia, and second malignancies are late effects.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver, Principles and Practice of Radiation Therapy

### PIM-26 · _recall_
Cancer-related fatigue during radiation therapy is best characterized as:

- **A.** A rare and abnormal finding requiring immediate ICU admission
- **B.** A sign that treatment must always be stopped
- **C.** Identical to ordinary tiredness relieved fully by one night of sleep
- **D.** A common, often cumulative symptom managed with energy conservation and supportive care

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Fatigue is one of the most common treatment-related symptoms, often accumulating over the course, and is managed with energy conservation, activity, and supportive measures. It usually does not require stopping treatment.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / Washington & Leaver

### PIM-27 · _application_
A patient rates pain at 8/10 during setup. The most appropriate therapist response is to:

- **A.** Pause, assess the pain, adjust comfort/positioning as able, and notify the care team for management
- **B.** Tell the patient to tolerate it for the few minutes of treatment

- **D.** Document the pain only and proceed without action

## Comfort, Dignity, Transfers & Body Mechanics

### PIM-28 · _recall_
Maintaining patient modesty and dignity during treatment includes:

- **A.** Leaving the patient fully exposed for staff convenience
- **B.** Using gowns/drapes and exposing only the area required for setup
- **C.** Discussing the patient's condition loudly in the hallway
- **D.** Allowing observers without the patient's knowledge

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** Dignity is preserved by draping and exposing only the treatment area as needed. Unnecessary exposure, public discussion, or undisclosed observers violate dignity and privacy.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / Patient Bill of Rights

### PIM-29 · _recall_
A dependent transfer differs from an assisted transfer in that the patient:

- **A.** Can bear most of their own weight and needs only minor help
- **B.** Transfers entirely without any staff present
- **C.** Cannot bear weight or assist and relies fully on staff/equipment
- **D.** Only requires verbal cueing

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** In a dependent transfer the patient cannot bear weight or assist, requiring full staff and/or mechanical lift support. An assisted transfer involves a patient who can partially participate.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / safe patient handling guidance

### PIM-30 · _application_
When lifting or moving a patient, proper body mechanics require the therapist to:

- **A.** Keep the back straight, bend the knees, and keep the load close to the body
- **B.** Bend at the waist and lift with the back
- **C.** Twist the spine while turning the patient
- **D.** Reach far across the table to grasp the patient

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Safe body mechanics use a wide base, bent knees, a straight back, and the load held close to reduce spinal strain. Bending at the waist, twisting, or overreaching increases injury risk.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / OSHA safe patient handling

### PIM-31 · _application_
While moving a patient who has a Foley catheter and an IV line, the therapist should:

- **A.** Pull on the lines to keep them out of the way
- **B.** Clamp the IV permanently before the move
- **C.** Keep the drainage bag below bladder level, secure lines, and prevent tension or dislodgement
- **D.** Disconnect all lines without notifying nursing

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Lines and tubes must be secured and free of tension, with the urinary drainage bag kept below bladder level to prevent backflow. Pulling, clamping, or disconnecting without coordination risks harm.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / institutional line-management policy

### PIM-32 · _application_
A patient receiving supplemental oxygen at 2 L/min via nasal cannula must be moved to the treatment couch. The therapist should:

- **A.** Remove the oxygen for the duration of treatment
- **B.** Increase the flow to 10 L/min to be safe
- **C.** Maintain the prescribed oxygen flow and keep tubing free of kinks during the move
- **D.** Switch the source without checking the prescribed rate

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Prescribed oxygen flow must be maintained and tubing protected from kinks during transfers. Altering or discontinuing the ordered flow can harm the patient.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver / oxygen administration guidance

## Immobilization & Setup Compliance

### PIM-33 · _recall_
A thermoplastic mask used for head-and-neck immobilization primarily functions to:

- **A.** Deliver a measured dose of contrast
- **B.** Shield the brain from all scatter radiation
- **C.** Provide reproducible positioning and limit motion during treatment
- **D.** Replace the need for daily imaging

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Thermoplastic masks provide reproducible immobilization and limit motion for accurate setup. They are not shielding or imaging substitutes.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver, Principles and Practice of Radiation Therapy

### PIM-34 · _application_
A claustrophobic patient becomes anxious when the thermoplastic mask is applied. The best initial approach is to:

- **A.** Force the mask on quickly to get it over with
- **B.** Cancel radiation therapy as an option
- **C.** Explain the process, allow acclimation, use calming techniques, and coordinate anxiolytics with the physician if needed
- **D.** Tell the patient claustrophobia is not real

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Anxiety with mask immobilization is managed with explanation, acclimation, and calming techniques, escalating to the physician for anxiolytics when necessary. Forcing the device worsens distress and compromises setup.
**Source:** ASRT Practice Standards / Washington & Leaver

### PIM-35 · _recall_
A vacuum (vac-bag) immobilization device works by:

- **A.** Inflating around the patient to apply pressure
- **B.** Conforming to the patient's contour and hardening when air is evacuated
- **C.** Emitting a low-dose radiation field
- **D.** Cooling the skin to reduce reaction

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A vac-bag molds to the patient and becomes rigid when air is removed, giving a reproducible custom contour. It does not inflate, irradiate, or cool the patient.
**Source:** Washington & Leaver, Principles and Practice of Radiation Therapy

## Medical Emergencies & BLS/ACLS Readiness

### PIM-36 · _application_
A patient suddenly collapses, is unresponsive, and is not breathing normally. The therapist's first priorities are to:

- **A.** Finish documentation, then act
- **B.** Move the patient to a private room first
- **C.** Wait for the physician before doing anything
- **D.** Activate the emergency response, check for a pulse, and begin CPR with high-quality chest compressions as indicated

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** For an unresponsive, apneic patient, the therapist activates emergency response and begins high-quality chest compressions per BLS. Delaying for documentation or relocation reduces survival.
**Source:** AHA BLS guidelines

### PIM-37 · _recall_
Current AHA BLS guidelines recommend a chest-compression rate of approximately:

- **A.** 100–120 per minute
- **B.** 40–60 per minute
- **C.** 150–180 per minute
- **D.** As fast as possible without limit

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** High-quality CPR uses a compression rate of 100–120 per minute with adequate depth and full recoil. Slower or excessively fast rates reduce effectiveness.
**Source:** AHA BLS guidelines

### PIM-38 · _application_
A patient reports sudden crushing chest pain radiating to the left arm during treatment. The therapist should:

- **A.** Continue treatment and reassess tomorrow
- **B.** Give the patient aspirin from the staff break room
- **C.** Stop treatment, keep the patient still, activate emergency response, and summon the physician/nurse immediately
- **D.** Have the patient walk to the waiting area

**Answer:** C
**Explanation:** Chest pain suggestive of cardiac ischemia requires stopping treatment, minimizing exertion, and activating emergency response with immediate physician/nurse involvement. Therapists do not independently administer medications.
**Source:** AHA ACLS readiness / institutional emergency protocol

### PIM-39 · _recall_
Standard supplemental oxygen for an emergency in the department is most commonly delivered initially via:

- **A.** Intravenous infusion
- **B.** Oral tablet
- **C.** Nasal cannula or non-rebreather mask, per the patient's need and orders
- **D.** Rectal suppository

## Iodinated Contrast Media

### PIM-40 · _recall_
Compared with ionic contrast media, non-ionic iodinated contrast agents are associated with:

- **A.** Higher osmolality and more adverse reactions
- **B.** No iodine content
- **C.** Greater risk of allergic reactions in all patients
- **D.** Lower osmolality and a lower incidence of adverse reactions

**Answer:** D
**Explanation:** Non-ionic, low-osmolality agents have a lower incidence of adverse reactions than higher-osmolality ionic agents. Both contain iodine.
**Source:** ACR Manual on Contrast Media

Before iodinated contrast administration at CT simulation, which screening items are most important?

- **D.** Marital status and occupation

**Answer:** B

### PIM-42 · _application_
Shortly after iodinated contrast injection, a patient develops widespread hives, wheezing, and hypotension. This presentation is most consistent with a:

- **A.** Minor reaction requiring only observation

### PIM-43 · _recall_
Which is an example of a MINOR (mild) iodinated contrast reaction generally requiring observation only?

## Ethics, Law & Infection Control

### PIM-44 · _application_
A patient with decision-making capacity refuses a scheduled treatment. The therapist's most appropriate action is to:

- **A.** Treat the patient anyway because the plan is already approved
- **B.** Respect the patient's autonomy, document the refusal, and notify the physician
- **C.** Tell the patient refusal is not allowed once consent is signed
- **D.** Have security compel the patient onto the table

**Answer:** B
**Explanation:** A competent patient has the right to refuse care; the therapist respects autonomy, documents the refusal, and notifies the physician. Coercion violates patient rights and ethics.
**Source:** Patient Bill of Rights / ARRT Standards of Ethics

### PIM-45 · _application_
A therapist must care for a patient on neutropenic precautions and another on contact precautions. Which statement is correct?

- **A.** Standard Precautions apply to all patients, with neutropenic (protective) precautions shielding the patient and contact precautions preventing organism spread
- **B.** Hand hygiene is optional if gloves are worn
- **C.** PPE is unnecessary for transmission-based precautions
- **D.** Neutropenic and contact precautions are identical in purpose

**Answer:** A
**Explanation:** Standard Precautions apply universally; neutropenic (protective) precautions protect the immunocompromised patient, whereas contact precautions limit organism transmission. Hand hygiene is always required, even with gloves.
**Source:** CDC Standard and Transmission-Based Precautions
